masculinity and power of money
(as can be connected to Mr.R and Antoinette)

Throughout Trust, money is wielded as a weapon. Bevel leverages as a successful male measured by modern societal standard uses his wealth to manipulate markets, controls narratives, and distances himself from others. The novel shows that immense wealth can be a corrupting force, distorting one's sense of reality and altering empathy.
Additionally, it creates an illusion of meritocracy. Bevel's rise to power remains ambiguous because he controls the narratives. The novel suggests multiple times about the corrupting power of money. "Capital may be clean, but Rask's hands are dirty with the plight of the nation..." (My life) The fragmented narratives make it unclear if his wealth stemmed from genuine skill or exploitation. This ambiguity suggests that the wealthy elite might not always deserve their place at the top.

fragmented narratives in modern life

With money and power, Andrew Bevel can shape the narrative and create a web of lies. Each section of the novel offers a conflicting perspective on Bevel, making it impossible to discern the absolute truth. This reveals the difficulty to trust any single narrator and exposes the subjectivity inherent in historical narratives.
Bevel attempts to control his image through his unfinished autobiography, but it's ultimately challenged by the other narratives- Ida Partenza, and particularly his wife Mildred Bevel. This dynamic highlights the struggle for control over historical narratives, especially when wealth and power are involved. As revealed by Ida Partnow, "My job is about being right. Always. If I'm ever wrong, I must make use of all my means and resources to bend and align reality according to my mistake so that it ceases to be a mistake." This chilling quote from the financial journalist exposes the lengths to which some might go to control narratives.
The fragmented structure forces the reader to become an active participant, piecing together the truth from various perspectives. This emphasizes the importance of considering multiple viewpoints when evaluating the actions of powerful figures in modern life, when single narrative is dicarded.

masculinity and class: stark divide in modern life

The novel brings readers the attention to Mildred(Bevel's wife)'s Silenced Voice Mildred's struggles to find her own agency within their marriage. Her perspective exposes the limitations placed on women of her class during this era. Her voice is often silenced or marginalized, reflecting the societal power structures that favor wealthy men.
Additionally, it discuess the modern society puts the Working Class in the Shadows. The novel primarily focuses on the upper class, but the wealth they enjoy is built on the labor of the working class. Their stories remain untold, highlighting the vast gulf between the wealthy elite and those who enable their success.

This pursuit of the "ideal man" takes a toll on his emotional well-being and his relationships. Another character Septimus Warren Smith is a victim of war trauma. He also suffers from toxic masculinity expectation. Septimus represents the fragility of the traditional masculine ideal. compare and contrast

Trust and Mrs. Dalloway, despite their differing contexts, offer a critical look at the concept of toxic masculinity. They expose the damage it can inflict on individuals and society, prompting reflections on how we can redefine masculinity to be more inclusive.
differences
Focus: Trust critiques the ruthlessness and moral ambiguity of masculinity within the context of financial capitalism. Mrs. Dalloway focuses on the emotional repression and societal pressures associated with masculinity in the aftermath of war.
Historical Context: Trust reflects the changing definitions of masculinity during the industrial revolution. Mrs. Dalloway portrays the societal constraints placed on men in the wake of World War I.
similarity
Isolation and Unhappiness: Both novels depict the negative consequences of toxic masculinity. The characters who rigidly adhere to these ideals end up isolated and unhappy.
A Call for Change: By exposing the flaws of these masculine ideals, both novels implicitly call for a more nuanced and less restrictive definition of masculinity, allowing men to express emotions and vulnerability.
